Am I Myopic? I See Blurry Far Away

By 2050, half of humanity could be myopic, according to a study published in the journal Ophthalmology. Myopia is the most common visual defect and typically begins around age 6-8 before stabilising around 20-25 years. From that age, refractive surgery can be considered.

The causes of myopia are both genetic and environmental. Recent studies suggest that a lack of natural light and outdoor activities during childhood is a contributing factor.

A myopic person loses sharpness progressively with distance. A myope has difficulty seeing far but sees well up close. The cause? An excessive growth of the eye that becomes too long, not in terms of a standard, but relative to the distance at which the anterior tissues of the eye (cornea and lens) focus light rays. As a result, the image is formed in front of the retina instead of on it.

Laser Surgery for Myopia

The correction of myopia is usually achieved with a divergent lens or contact lens. The goal is to redirect light to focus it on the retina. However, distance vision is improved but the defect is merely compensated.

To truly correct myopia, laser eye surgery can be performed. The cornea is reshaped by reducing its radius of curvature.

There are several techniques for operating on myopia with laser, including SMILE Pro. SMILE Pro is the reference technique for myopia and astigmatism and involves creating a lenticule within the cornea which is then removed through a small incision.

You can return to your daily activities and work the following day. The suitability of laser surgery will be decided during your pre-operative assessment at our vision centre, based on your visual acuity, pupil size, corneal thickness and regularity.

Both eyes are operated on the same day, under local anaesthesia, on an outpatient basis. You can combine myopia surgery with astigmatism treatment, or even myopia with presbyopia.

In 2017, more than 150,000 myopic people in the UK opted for refractive surgery!
